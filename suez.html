Geographic Context

The Suez Canal and the Red Sea are two of the world's most important maritime routes, accounting for 10% of global trade and 12% of oil shipments worldwide. Southbound traffic must pass through the 26-kilometre-wide Bab-el-Mandeb Strait, also known as the 'Gate of Tears', to reach the Indian Ocean. As this route is integral to global supply chains, any military or physical blockage instantly cuts off the main shipping route between Europe and Asia. This forces global shipping to find alternative routes.

Historic Timeline

March 2021 (The Physical Blockage): The Ever Given container ship runs aground, blocking the canal entirely. This proved how easily a single physical event can disrupt global supply chains.
Present (The Security Blockage): Escalating Houthi militant attacks on vessels in the Bab-el-Mandeb Strait have turned the region into an active conflict zone. This effectively compromises the safety of the corridor.
The Rerouting Solution: To ensure crew and cargo safety, major shipping lines assess security risks dynamically. They reroute vessels around the southern tip of Africa via the Cape of Good Hope when security while transiting the Red Sea cannot be guaranteed.

Strategic Importance

Forcing vessels to take the massive detour around the Cape of Good Hope has severe effects on national economies. Adding 10 or more days to a voyage increases fuel consumption, disrupts port schedules and causes supply disruptions. All of these factors act as direct drivers of global inflation.
