The Fragile Artery

The Suez Canal is a critical global artery responsible for handling 10-15% of global trade[cite: 124]. Extending south from the canal, maritime traffic must pass through the Bab-El-Mandeb strait (the "Gate of Tears") to reach the Indian Ocean[cite: 124]. The closure or restriction of the Bab-El-Mandeb Strait prevents tankers originating in the Persian Gulf from transiting the Suez Canal or reaching the SUMED Pipeline[cite: 126].

Geopolitical Disruption

March 2021: The Ever Given container ship runs aground, blocking the canal entirely and spotlighting the fragile nature of global just-in-time supply chains[cite: 128].
Present: Escalating Houthi attacks in the Red Sea have created a scenario of "Accessibility under Siege"[cite: 128, 129].
Rerouting Impact: To avoid the conflict zone, maritime vessels are forced to divert around the southern tip of Africa via the Cape of Good Hope, adding 10 or more days to delivery times[cite: 132, 134].

Economic Spillover

The contrast between the accessible Suez route and the alternative Cape of Good Hope route has a severe "spillover" effect on national economies[cite: 132, 133]. The extended voyage distance significantly increases transit times, maritime fuel costs, and massive spikes in insurance premiums, driving global inflation[cite: 126, 133].
