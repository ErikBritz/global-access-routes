Geographic Context

The Suez Canal is a critical global artery responsible for handling 10-15% of global trade. Extending south from the canal, maritime traffic must pass through the Bab-El-Mandeb strait (the "Gate of Tears") to reach the Indian Ocean. The closure or restriction of the Bab-El-Mandeb Strait prevents tankers originating in the Persian Gulf from transiting the Suez Canal or reaching the SUMED Pipeline.

Historic Timeline

March 2021: The Ever Given container ship runs aground, blocking the canal entirely and spotlighting how fragile global supply chains are.
Present: Escalating Houthi attacks in the Red Sea have resulted in a situation where accessibility is under threat.
Rerouting Impact: To avoid the conflict zone, maritime vessels are forced to divert around the southern tip of Africa via the Cape of Good Hope, adding 10 or more days to delivery times.

Cape of Good Hope Rerouting Impact

As security threats in the Red Sea escalate, major global shipping lines are forced to reroute their ships dynamically to avoid active conflict zones. This seven-day moving average (in million tons) illustrates the relationship between the decline in Red Sea transits and the corresponding increase in the number of vessels taking the lengthy and expensive detour around Africa's Cape of Good Hope.

Impact on Global Trade

The difference in accessibility between the Suez route and the alternative Cape of Good Hope route has a severe spillover effect on national economies. The increased distance of the voyage significantly raises transit times and maritime fuel costs, as well as causing massive spikes in insurance premiums, thereby driving global inflation.
